Complete hair science for African textures — for women with natural, relaxed, or transitioning hair, and for men managing waves, locs, fades, and scalp health.

Curl Typing

Understanding your curl type — 3a to 4c

The curl typing system describes the shape and pattern of your hair strands. For African hair, the most relevant types are 3c through 4c — but understanding where you sit changes what products you reach for and how you build your routine.

3C
Tight Corkscrews
Very tight corkscrews with lots of volume. Dense, prone to shrinkage. Still has a defined curl pattern without product. Common in mixed-texture and transitioning hair.
Defined without product High shrinkage Dense
4A
Tight Coils
Tight coils with a visible S-pattern when stretched. Retains moisture reasonably well compared to 4b and 4c. Shrinks significantly but maintains some definition.
S-pattern Moderate moisture retention ~50% shrinkage
4B
Z-Pattern Coil
Z-shaped coil with very little curl definition without product. Very dense. Shrinks up to 75%. Extremely common in Nigeria and Ghana. Requires intentional moisture layering.
Z-pattern Very common in West Africa ~75% shrinkage
4C
Tightest Coil
The tightest coil — almost no visible pattern without manipulation. Highest shrinkage of all types. The most common texture across West and East Africa. The most underserved by mainstream hair content.
Most common in Africa ~80% shrinkage Most underserved
3A
Loose Spirals
Loose, well-defined spirals. Shiny, bouncy, and the least prone to dryness of the curly types. Less common in West Africa but relevant for the diaspora and mixed-texture hair.
Loose spiral Low shrinkage Retains moisture well
3B
Springy Curls
Tighter spirals with more volume than 3a. Still well-defined without product. More prone to frizz in humidity. Common in transitioning or heat-treated natural hair.
Springy Frizz-prone in humidity Medium shrinkage

Note: Most people have multiple curl types across their head. Your nape is often a different type to your crown. Build your routine around your most difficult section.

Porosity

Why your hair rejects moisture — and how to fix it

Porosity is how well your hair absorbs and retains water and product. It's determined by the state of your cuticle layer. Getting this wrong means your hair stays dry no matter how much you put on it — or it gets weighed down constantly.

Low Porosity
Cuticles tightly shut
Water and product sit on the hair instead of absorbing. Takes forever to get fully wet. Products build up on the surface over time. Common in very healthy, unprocessed 4c hair.

Signs

  • Hair takes long to get wet
  • Products sit on hair, don't absorb
  • Hair dries slowly
  • Product buildup despite using little
Medium Porosity
Cuticles balanced
The ideal state. Hair absorbs moisture easily and holds it well. Styles hold, products work as expected, and routines are forgiving. Most routines are written for this porosity type.

Signs

  • Hair gets wet quickly
  • Products absorb and dry at normal rate
  • Holds styles without too much effort
  • Responds well to most products
High Porosity
Cuticles raised / damaged
Absorbs moisture very fast but loses it just as fast. Hair dries quickly, feels rough, tangles easily. Usually the result of heat damage, chemical processing, or hard water damage over time.

Signs

  • Hair absorbs water immediately
  • Dries very fast after washing
  • Always feels dry despite moisturising
  • Tangles and breaks easily

The Float Test

A simple at-home test to determine your porosity. Do this on clean, product-free hair for the most accurate result.

  • 01Wash your hair with a clarifying shampoo and do not add any product
  • 02Let a few strands air dry completely
  • 03Drop the strands into a glass of room-temperature water
  • 04Wait 2–4 minutes without disturbing the glass
  • 05Floats = low porosity · Sinks slowly = medium · Sinks fast = high

Damage

What's actually breaking your hair

Most hair damage comes from one of six causes. Knowing which one is yours changes everything about how you treat it.

Heat Damage
Permanent change to your curl pattern from flat irons, blow dryers, and hot combs applied too frequently or at too high a temperature. The hair does not bounce back. Straightened sections must be cut out over time.
PermanentCurl pattern lossMust grow out
Chemical Damage
Relaxers, texturisers, and dyes alter the hair's protein structure. Overlapping relaxer on already-processed hair is the most common cause of severe breakage in Nigerian women.
Protein structure changeOverlapping relaxersDye damage
Physical Breakage
Tight braids, rubber bands at the same spot repeatedly, harsh detangling, and manipulation when hair is dry. The edges and nape are the most vulnerable. Traction alopecia is the permanent version of this.
Traction alopeciaEdge lossOver-manipulation
Hard Water Damage
Extremely relevant in Lagos, Abuja, and many Nigerian cities. The minerals in hard water (calcium, magnesium) coat the hair shaft, block moisture absorption, cause dullness, and accelerate breakage over time. Your routine may not be failing — your water might be.
Lagos-specificMineral buildupClarify monthly
Hygral Fatigue
Over-wetting the hair causes the cuticle to swell and contract repeatedly. Washing daily or keeping hair wet constantly weakens the strand structure. Particularly common with co-wash-only routines.
Over-washingCo-wash overuseCuticle swelling
Discolouration
Natural black hair turning reddish or brown is a signal — not a style. Causes include sun exposure (UV bleaching), hard water mineral deposits, protein deficiency, and chlorine from swimming pools. Each cause has a different fix.
Sun bleachingProtein deficiencyChlorine damage

Men's Hair

For men — waves, fades, braids, locs, and scalp

African men's hair covers every texture and style — from skin fades to braids to locs. This section covers what applies to men specifically.

Waves

360 waves require consistent brushing, moisture, and compression. The right brush hardness depends on your curl type — 4c hair needs softer bristles with more moisture. Over-brushing on dry hair causes breakage, not progress.

Low Cuts & Fades

Scalp health matters more when your hair is short. Dry scalp, ingrown hairs, and razor bumps are common. Moisturising the scalp directly — not just the hair — and using a gentle exfoliant weekly prevents most issues.

Locs

Locs need moisture throughout — not just at the scalp. Buildup from heavy products inside loc strands causes the same issues as product buildup on loose hair. Clarify periodically, keep the scalp clean, and avoid over-retwisting which causes thinning at the root.

Skin Fade & Taper

The skin fade starts at the skin at the sides and back and gradually blends into length on top. The taper is softer — it drops down without fully reaching skin. Skin fades suit round and wide head shapes; tapers are more forgiving on flat-back heads. Both require a barber who understands African hair texture — clipper settings and technique differ from European hair.

Braided Hair

Braids on men have deep roots across West, East, and Central Africa — cornrows, single braids, fulani braids, and box braids are all part of the tradition, not a trend. They work from 4 inches upward. Braids are a protective style — they reduce manipulation and breakage while your hair grows. Keep the scalp moisturised and avoid leaving braids in past 6–8 weeks to prevent matting at the root.

Beard & Follicle Health

The same follicle science that applies to scalp hair applies to your beard. Ingrown hairs, patchiness, and dry skin under the beard are all addressable — and connected to how you treat your skin, not just your beard products.

Beard Types

African beard types — and how to work with yours

African beard hair tends to be coarser, curlier, and more prone to ingrown hairs than European beard types. Understanding your beard's growth pattern is the first step to grooming it properly.

Full Beard
All-over coverage
Even growth across the chin, jaw, cheeks, and upper lip. The most versatile beard type. African full beards are typically denser and curlier than their counterparts — requiring regular moisture to prevent dryness and breakage.
Moisturise daily with beard oil. Trim every 2 weeks to maintain shape. Condition with shea butter or a curl cream to keep coils defined.
Goatee
Chin and mustache only
Hair on the chin and upper lip only, with clean-shaved cheeks. Excellent for men whose cheek growth is patchy. Balances round and full faces by elongating the chin. Very common in West African men.
Clean shave the cheeks every 2–3 days. Keep the goatee edges sharp. Works well with both fades and longer hair styles.
Stubble
Short, even growth
1–3mm of growth kept deliberately short and even. One of the most flattering beard types for most face shapes. Hides patchiness well and requires minimal maintenance. The right clipper guard makes all the difference.
Use a 1–2 guard for light stubble. Moisturise the skin underneath — not just the beard. Fade into the hairline for a cleaner finish.
Patchy Beard
Uneven growth pattern
Sparse or uneven growth in some areas — very common among African men. Often genetic, not a grooming failure. The right approach is working with the patches, not against them. Keeping it shorter makes patchiness less noticeable.
Keep length short to even out density. Focus shaping on your strongest growth areas. Beard derma rollers and biotin can stimulate sparse areas over time.
Low Fade Beard
Faded into the hairline
The beard fades seamlessly into a low or mid haircut — no clear line between hair and beard. One of the sharpest looking styles for African men. Requires a skilled barber and regular upkeep every 1–2 weeks.
Ask your barber specifically for a beard fade, not just a lineup. The fade should connect through the sideburns and temple. Don't let it grow out more than 10 days or the fade blurs.
Coily / Natural Beard
Unmanipulated coil growth
Letting the beard grow in its natural coil pattern without heat or chemical straightening. African beard coils are some of the most distinct and textured in the world. Requires consistent moisture to prevent dryness and knots.
Detangle while wet using a wide-tooth comb or brush. Use a leave-in conditioner or beard butter. Avoid over-brushing when dry — it causes frizz and breakage.

Face Shapes

Your face shape — and what it means for your hair

Face shape is the single most useful factor when choosing a hairstyle. African facial structures cover the full spectrum — but proportions differ from the Western reference. Use this as a guide, not a rule.

Oval
Balanced · Gently rounded chin
Forehead slightly wider than the jaw with a softly rounded chin. The most versatile face shape — almost every hairstyle works. The reference most style guides assume as default.

Recommended styles

  • Wash-and-go afros, twist-outs, bantu knots — all flattering
  • Braids worn up, down, or half-up work equally well
  • Locs of any length sit beautifully on this shape
  • Experiment freely — very little to avoid here
Round
Equal width and length · Soft jaw
Width and length are roughly equal with a soft, curved jaw. Common in West African women. The goal is to add length and create the illusion of a slimmer frame.

Recommended styles

  • High buns and pineapple updos to add vertical height
  • Long braids or locs past the chin to elongate the face
  • Centre parts — avoid side parts that widen
  • Avoid wide, voluminous afros that add horizontal width
Square
Strong jaw · Angular · Equal width
Strong, defined jawline with similar width at the forehead and jaw. A powerful, structured look common across sub-Saharan women. The goal is to soften the angles with movement and texture.

Recommended styles

  • Loose twist-outs and curl definition — soft texture at the jaw
  • Side-swept braids or locs that break the jaw's straight line
  • Layers and volume at the crown to draw the eye upward
  • Avoid blunt bobs or straight cuts that mirror the jaw angle
Oblong / Rectangle
Long · Narrow · Even width
Face is noticeably longer than it is wide with consistent width throughout. Common in East African women — Kenyan, Ethiopian, Somali frames often carry this shape. The goal is to add width and break the vertical line.

Recommended styles

  • Wide afros with volume at the sides, not just height
  • Fringe and baby hairs to reduce the forehead's length
  • Short to medium bobs — shoulder-length styles look excellent
  • Avoid very long styles worn straight down
Diamond
Narrow forehead · Wide cheekbones · Narrow chin
Widest at the cheekbones, tapering to both a narrow forehead and chin. High cheekbones are a prominent African feature. The goal is to balance the forehead and soften the pointed chin.

Recommended styles

  • Volume or width at the crown to widen the forehead
  • Chin-length styles that add mass to the lower face
  • Wispy or feathered ends at the chin
  • Avoid styles with maximum volume at the cheekbones
Heart
Wide forehead · Narrow jaw
Wide forehead tapering to a pointed or narrow chin. Present across the continent, especially in women with high foreheads. The goal is to reduce forehead width and add weight at the lower face.

Recommended styles

  • Side parts and swept styles to break up the forehead
  • Volume and fullness at the jaw and chin
  • Chin-length bobs, lob styles, or braids at the chin
  • Avoid centre-parted styles with volume only at the top

Head Shapes

Head shape — what your barber considers first

Your head shape affects how a fade or shape-up sits from every angle. This is what a good barber accounts for before they pick up the clippers.

Round Head
Even curve all around
Evenly curved from all angles with no flat spots. The most common head shape across African men. Cuts sit naturally — the shape is forgiving and works with almost any fade level or top length. Most barbers plan cuts assuming this shape.

Best cuts

  • High fades with medium top length — very balanced
  • Natural afros with even shaping around the perimeter
  • Locs in any length — sit evenly all around
  • Skin fades look especially clean on this shape
Flat Back
Flat occipital region
A flat area at the back of the skull — very common and often unnoticed until a skin fade reveals it. Can make the back of a tight fade look uneven or square from the side. A skilled barber will feather the fade differently to compensate. Tell your barber upfront.

Best cuts

  • Taper fades — softer drop at the back, less exposure of the flat zone
  • Leaving some length at the back to fill and round it visually
  • Locs — add fullness and completely mask the shape
  • Avoid very close skin fades at the back — exposes the flatness
Long / Narrow
Longer front-to-back than side-to-side
Head is noticeably longer from front to back than it is wide side-to-side. Common in East African builds — Somali, Ethiopian, Eritrean frames often carry this shape. Adding width through the sides of a cut balances it; adding only height makes it more pronounced.

Best cuts

  • Medium fades that keep volume through the sides
  • Afros shaped for width, not just height
  • Temp fades or low fades — preserve side width
  • Avoid very short all-over cuts and high skin fades — expose the length
Wide / Broad
Wider than it is long
Head is wider side-to-side than front-to-back. Common in broader West African frames. Adding vertical height through the top of a cut elongates the head shape visually. Skin fades are very effective here because they remove width at the sides.

Best cuts

  • High skin fades — remove side width cleanly
  • Vertical growth on top — flat tops, high-top fades, afros with height
  • Locs worn up or piled high — add vertical presence
  • Avoid cuts that add maximum volume at the sides

Accessories

What to wear with your hair — accessories that actually fit

Accessories for natural hair, locs, and African head shapes. What works with your texture, curl type, and head shape — and what to avoid.

Head Wraps & Gele
All hair types · Women
Ankara and Aso-oke head wraps are the most versatile accessory for African hair. They work on every texture from 3a to 4c, protect hair on lazy days or protective style transitions, and can be styled dozens of ways. Gele is the structured formal version for events.

Works best for

  • 4c and 4b hair — protects and adds visual drama
  • Transitioning hair — hides two textures during the grow-out
  • Bad hair days — always looks intentional
  • Any face shape — can be tied to add or reduce height
Durag & Wave Cap
Short hair · Men · Waves
Essential tool for 360 waves — not just a style accessory. The durag compresses the hair against the scalp after brushing, locking in the wave pattern. Satin durags are better than velvet for moisture retention and avoiding frizz.

Works best for

  • Short natural hair working on wave patterns
  • After moisturising and brushing — locks in the work
  • Overnight — keeps edges laid while sleeping
  • Round and oval head shapes — the most secure fit
Satin Bonnet
All textures · Women · Night
Not optional for natural hair — cotton pillowcases draw moisture out of African hair overnight and cause friction that leads to breakage and frizz. A satin bonnet is the single cheapest habit that improves hair health immediately.

Works best for

  • 3c–4c textures — highest moisture loss without protection
  • Protective styles — keeps braids and twists fresh longer
  • Wash-and-go styles — preserves curl definition overnight
Loc Jewellery & Cuffs
Locs · Men & Women
Metal cuffs, beads, and wraps for locs — a tradition with roots across West and East Africa. Modern loc jewellery ranges from minimal metal rings to elaborate cowrie shell and bead arrangements. They add visual interest without damaging the locs when fitted correctly.

Works best for

  • Medium to long locs — enough length to hold the piece
  • Starter locs — beads add visual weight to thin new locs
  • Oblong and oval face shapes — draw the eye across the locs
  • Avoid very tight cuffs that compress the loc at the root
Hair Pins & Clips
Natural & relaxed · Women
Bobby pins and hair clips work differently on African hair — the coil pattern means more grip is needed and standard pins can slip. Spiral pins and duckbill clips with rubberised grips are significantly more effective for 4b and 4c textures. Avoid metal pins with rough ends on fine hair.

Works best for

  • Pinning updos and twists in place without slipping
  • Sectioning during wash day
  • 3a–3c hair — standard pins work reasonably well
  • 4b–4c — use spiral pins or large jaw clips instead
Face Cap
Short hair · Men · Everyday
The face cap — snapback, dad hat, or fitted — is the most universal men's hair accessory. Works as protection from sun, wind, and dust. How it sits on your head depends on your head shape and haircut; high fades and low cuts pair cleanest with fitted caps.

Works best for

  • Round and oval head shapes — sits evenly without gaps
  • Low cuts and skin fades — the cleanest pairing
  • Waves — a fitted cap compresses and sets the pattern
  • Avoid very high-top styles — the crown sits too high for most caps
