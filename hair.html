Curl Typing

Understanding your curl type — 3a to 4c

The curl typing system was created in the 90s to describe pattern and structure. It's imperfect — most people sit between types, and your nape will often be different to your crown. But knowing your curl type still matters, because it changes which products actually work and how you sequence your routine.

3C
Tight Corkscrews
Very tight corkscrews with serious volume. Dense, shrinks significantly, but still holds a visible pattern without product. Common in transitioning hair and mixed African textures.
Defined without product High shrinkage Dense
4A
Tight Coils
Tight coils with a visible S-pattern when you stretch them out. Better at holding moisture than 4b or 4c. Still shrinks a lot — but unlike 4b and 4c, you'll usually see definition even without product.
S-pattern Moderate moisture retention ~50% shrinkage
4B
Z-Pattern Coil
Z-shaped coil — looks almost shrunk-flat without product. Very dense and very common across West Africa. Shrinks up to 75%. The pattern becomes visible once you add moisture and define it, but you have to earn it.
Z-pattern Very common in West Africa ~75% shrinkage
4C
Tightest Coil
The tightest coil — no visible pattern until you manipulate it. Highest shrinkage of any type, often 80% or more. The most common texture across West and East Africa, and the most consistently ignored by mainstream hair content.
Most common in Africa ~80% shrinkage Most underserved
3A
Loose Spirals
Loose, well-defined spirals that hold their shape easily. Shiny, bouncy, and significantly more forgiving with moisture than the 4 types. Less common in West Africa — more relevant for diaspora and mixed-texture hair.
Loose spiral Low shrinkage Retains moisture well
3B
Springy Curls
Tighter spirals with more volume than 3a. Still holds definition without much product. Frizzes more noticeably in humidity. You'll often find this type in hair that's transitioning from relaxed or heat-treated.
Springy Frizz-prone in humidity Medium shrinkage

Most people have more than one curl type across their head — your nape and crown are often completely different. Build your routine around the section that needs the most work, not the most cooperative one.

Porosity

Why your hair rejects moisture — and how to fix it

Porosity is how easily your hair takes in and holds onto water. It's controlled by the state of your cuticle — the outer layer of each strand. If you've ever piled product onto your hair and it still felt dry an hour later, porosity is the explanation. Get this right and everything else in your routine gets easier.

Low Porosity
Cuticles tightly shut
Water and product sit on the surface instead of soaking in. Your hair takes a long time to get fully wet. Buildup happens fast even with small amounts of product. Ironically, this is common in very healthy, unprocessed 4c hair — the cuticles are tight because nothing has damaged them.

Signs

  • Hair takes long to get wet
  • Products sit on hair, don't absorb
  • Hair dries slowly
  • Product buildup despite using little
Medium Porosity
Cuticles balanced
The sweet spot. Hair takes in moisture easily and keeps it. Products work like they're supposed to, styles hold, and your routine doesn't need to be complicated. Almost every hair product and routine guide is written with this porosity in mind.

Signs

  • Hair gets wet quickly
  • Products absorb and dry at normal rate
  • Holds styles without too much effort
  • Responds well to most products
High Porosity
Cuticles raised / damaged
Soaks up water instantly but loses it just as quickly. Hair dries fast, feels rough, tangles easily. This is usually damage — from heat tools, chemical processing, or prolonged hard water exposure. The cuticle is lifted and can't close properly to seal moisture in.

Signs

  • Hair absorbs water immediately
  • Dries very fast after washing
  • Always feels dry despite moisturising
  • Tangles and breaks easily

The Float Test

A simple at-home test to determine your porosity. Do this on clean, product-free hair for the most accurate result.

  • 01Wash your hair with a clarifying shampoo and do not add any product
  • 02Let a few strands air dry completely
  • 03Drop the strands into a glass of room-temperature water
  • 04Wait 2–4 minutes without disturbing the glass
  • 05Floats = low porosity · Sinks slowly = medium · Sinks fast = high

Damage

What's actually breaking your hair

Most people treating damage are treating the wrong thing. There are six main causes, and the fix for each one is completely different. Knowing which one applies to you matters more than any product.

Heat Damage
Flat irons, blow dryers, and hot combs used too often or at too high a temperature permanently alter the curl pattern. The hair simply doesn't bounce back. Once a section is heat-damaged, the only real fix is cutting it out as new growth comes in — there's no treatment that reverses it.
PermanentCurl pattern lossMust grow out
Chemical Damage
Relaxers, texturisers, and dyes work by changing the hair's protein bonds — that's the point of them. The damage comes from overlapping: applying fresh relaxer over already-processed hair is the single most common cause of severe breakage. The edges of the relaxed section are the weakest point and break first.
Protein structure changeOverlapping relaxersDye damage
Physical Breakage
Tight braids, rubber bands left at the same spot, detangling dry hair too aggressively, and styling when the hair has no moisture — all of these pull or snap the strand. The edges and nape are most vulnerable because the hair there is finer. When the damage is repeated over a long period, the follicle itself stops producing hair. That's traction alopecia, and it doesn't grow back.
Traction alopeciaEdge lossOver-manipulation
Hard Water Damage
The minerals in hard water — primarily calcium and magnesium — coat the hair shaft over time, blocking moisture and causing dullness and brittleness. Highly relevant in Lagos, Abuja, and most Nigerian cities where tap water is hard. If you've changed your products and your routine still isn't working, test your water. A clarifying wash once a month removes most buildup.
Lagos-specificMineral buildupClarify monthly
Hygral Fatigue
Every time hair gets wet the cuticle swells, and every time it dries the cuticle contracts. Done too frequently, this repeated swelling and shrinking weakens the strand from the inside — the hair becomes fragile even without any external damage. Daily co-washing or keeping hair permanently wet are the most common causes.
Over-washingCo-wash overuseCuticle swelling
Discolouration
Black hair turning reddish or brown isn't a random occurrence — it's your hair telling you something is wrong. UV exposure bleaches the melanin in the hair shaft. Hard water deposits change how light reflects off the strand. Protein deficiency weakens the hair structure and can alter colour. Chlorine from pools strips melanin directly. Each cause needs a different fix, so identifying the right one matters.
Sun bleachingProtein deficiencyChlorine damage

The Journey

The shift from relaxed hair to natural hair

For many years, relaxed hair was the norm across Black communities — often because straighter hair was seen as more acceptable, easier to manage, or simply what everyone around you did. Over time, that started to change. Understanding why relaxers were so common, and what the shift away from them looks like, is the foundation of any honest natural hair guide.

Relaxed
Natural
Reason 01
Beauty standards and social pressure
Straighter hair was consistently held up by schools, workplaces, and mainstream media as more attractive and professional. That kind of repeated messaging creates real pressure — especially for young girls who had no representation of their natural texture being celebrated.
Reason 02
Convenience at a time of limited options
Relaxed hair was genuinely easier to manage when there were fewer products, tutorials, and salons built for natural textures. Detangling was simpler, styling time was shorter, and the routine felt predictable. That gap has closed significantly — but it was real.
Reason 03
It was just what everyone did
Many Black girls grew up watching their mothers, sisters, and aunties relax their hair. It became a normal part of the beauty routine before anyone questioned it. Normalisation is powerful — when something is all you've ever seen, it doesn't feel like a choice.
Reason 04
Avoiding judgment and discrimination
Some people relaxed their hair specifically to avoid negative comments about their natural texture — at school, at work, or in relationships. The decision was often less about preference and more about protection from environments that hadn't yet learned to accept textured hair.

The Hidden Cost

What repeated relaxing does to your hair

Relaxed hair being a cultural norm doesn't mean it was without cost. Frequent chemical relaxers, heat styling, and overlapping applications — done over years — leave real damage. Most people don't see it building until it's significant.

01
Breakage and thinning
The chemical bonds in the hair are permanently changed by relaxers. Overlapping fresh relaxer over previously processed sections — the most common mistake — breaks the hair at its weakest point. The edges and nape go first because the hair there is already finer.
02
Scalp damage and sensitivity
Chemical relaxers are alkaline — they work by breaking down the disulfide bonds in the hair shaft. That same chemistry can irritate or chemically burn the scalp if left on too long or applied too frequently. Repeated exposure weakens the scalp environment that the follicle depends on.
03
Loss of natural curl pattern
Long-term relaxing can make the natural curl pattern difficult or impossible to recover — even after stopping. The structural change is often permanent in the treated section. What you grow out from the root is still natural, but the relaxed ends are chemically altered for good.
04
Chronic dryness
Chemical processing raises the cuticle and makes it harder for the hair to retain moisture. Relaxed hair requires more product, more maintenance, and more frequent moisture replenishment than healthy natural hair — though many people don't realise the dryness is coming from the chemical process itself.

Restoring

How to restore damaged natural hair

There is no fast fix for chemical or heat damage. But there is a clear process — and the hair that grows from healthy follicles, with the right care, will be healthier than what came before.

01
Stop the processing
The first step is the most obvious one: stop the thing causing the damage. No more relaxers, no more overlapping applications, no more heat on compromised strands. The scalp and follicle need time to stabilise before anything else makes a difference.
02
Trim regularly
Damaged ends don't repair. They travel up the strand and cause more breakage the longer they stay. Regular trims — even small ones — remove the worst of it and let the healthy new growth become the majority of what's left.
03
Build around moisture
Deep conditioners, leave-in creams, and sealing oils should anchor your routine. Damaged hair has a lifted cuticle — it drinks moisture in quickly and loses it just as fast. The LOC method (liquid, oil, cream) was built for exactly this.
04
Use protein carefully
Protein treatments can temporarily patch the gaps in damaged hair structure. Carefully — too much protein without moisture makes hair stiff and prone to snapping. Use a protein treatment once a month and follow it immediately with a deep moisturising conditioner.
05
Wear protective styles
Braids, twists, buns, and tucked styles reduce daily manipulation and let the hair rest. They also protect the ends — where most breakage starts. Make sure the style isn't tight enough to pull at the edges.
06
Be patient with the timeline
Hair grows roughly 1.5cm a month. Significant damage takes months or years to fully grow out depending on how long it's been accumulating. Set honest expectations and track progress in six-month windows, not weeks.
The Option

The big chop — a fresh start

For many people transitioning from relaxed to natural hair, the big chop is the clearest way forward. It means cutting off most or all of the chemically processed hair and starting from healthy new growth. It can feel emotional — sometimes it is. But many people describe it as one of the most freeing decisions they've made. You stop managing the damage and start building from scratch.

Why people choose it
  • Removes heavily damaged hair in one step — no more managing two textures
  • Allows healthier hair growth from the root up
  • Helps restore natural curl patterns without the old processed ends pulling them down
  • Makes the care routine simpler — one texture, one set of needs
  • For many, it shifts the relationship with their hair entirely

Care

How to care for natural hair

Natural hair thrives on moisture, patience, and low manipulation. Every curl pattern is different — learning your specific hair is part of the process. These are the foundations that apply across all textures.

01
Keep it moisturised
Use leave-in conditioners, creams, or oils consistently. Natural hair — especially 4b and 4c — loses moisture fast. A daily or every-other-day moisture refresh is not excessive, it's standard.
02
Deep condition regularly
Once a week if your hair is damaged or dry, once every two weeks if it's healthy. Deep conditioners penetrate the shaft in a way that rinse-out conditioners don't. Don't skip this.
03
Avoid excessive heat
Straighteners and blow dryers on high heat permanently alter the curl pattern. If you use heat at all, use a heat protectant, keep the temperature below 230°C, and space it out — once a month at most.
04
Use protective styles
Braids, twists, buns, and wigs let the hair rest. They reduce daily manipulation and protect the ends — the oldest and most fragile part of the strand. Don't wear them so tight they pull at the edges.
05
Detangle gently and wet
Always detangle on damp hair with conditioner or a detangler. Use a wide-tooth comb or your fingers, starting at the ends and working upward. Dry detangling on natural hair causes breakage every time.
06
Protect at night
Cotton pillowcases pull moisture out of the hair and cause friction that leads to breakage. A satin bonnet or satin pillowcase is a straightforward fix — it keeps moisture in and reduces overnight frizz significantly.
07
Learn your porosity
Low porosity hair resists moisture. High porosity hair drinks it in but loses it fast. Medium is the easiest to manage. Your porosity — not just your curl pattern — determines which products actually work for you.
08
Trim when needed
Split ends travel up the strand and cause more breakage the longer they stay. You don't need to trim on a fixed schedule — trim when you feel rough, split, or snagging ends. Small consistent trims are better than waiting for major damage.
09
Be patient with the journey
Healthy natural hair takes time and consistency. The first year is the hardest — results aren't always visible immediately. Track your progress over months, not weeks. The hair that grows with consistent care is always better than what came before it.

Men's Hair

For men — waves, fades, braids, locs, and scalp

African men's hair covers the full range — from skin fades to freeform locs. The scalp advice and care principles from the women's section apply here too. This section covers what's specific to men's styles and concerns.

Waves

Waves are built through consistent brushing, moisture, and compression — in that order. The brush hardness you need depends on your curl type. If you have 4c hair, softer bristles and more moisture, not harder brushes. Brushing dry hair doesn't create waves, it creates breakage.

Low Cuts & Fades

Short hair puts the scalp on display, so scalp health matters more here than in any other style. Dry scalp, ingrown hairs, and razor bumps are common African skin concerns around the hairline. Moisturising the scalp directly — not just the hair — and a gentle weekly exfoliant prevents most of them.

Locs

Locs need moisture through the whole length — not just at the root. Heavy product buildup inside the loc over time causes the same issues as buildup on loose hair: dullness, weakened strands, and smell. Clarify periodically, keep the scalp clean between washes, and don't retwist too often — over-retwisting thins the loc at the root until it breaks.

Skin Fade & Taper

The skin fade starts at the skin at the sides and back and gradually blends into length on top. The taper is softer — it drops down without fully reaching skin. Skin fades suit round and wide head shapes; tapers are more forgiving on flat-back heads. Both require a barber who understands African hair texture — clipper settings and technique differ from European hair.

Braided Hair

Braids on men have deep roots across West, East, and Central Africa — cornrows, single braids, fulani braids, and box braids are all part of the tradition, not a trend. They work from 4 inches upward. Braids are a protective style — they reduce manipulation and breakage while your hair grows. Keep the scalp moisturised and avoid leaving braids in past 6–8 weeks to prevent matting at the root.

Beard & Follicle Health

The same follicle science that applies to scalp hair applies to your beard. Ingrown hairs, patchiness, and dry skin under the beard are all addressable — and connected to how you treat your skin, not just your beard products.

Beard Types

African beard types — and how to work with yours

African beard hair is typically coarser and curlier than European beard hair, which is why most mainstream grooming advice doesn't quite land. The curl pattern means ingrown hairs are more common and moisture is essential — not optional. Know your growth pattern first, then groom around it.

Full Beard
All-over coverage
Even growth across the chin, jaw, cheeks, and upper lip. The most versatile shape — works with most face types. African full beards are typically denser and curlier than European ones, which means dryness and breakage are real concerns if you skip moisture.
Moisturise daily with beard oil. Trim every 2 weeks to maintain shape. Condition with shea butter or a curl cream to keep coils defined.
Goatee
Chin and mustache only
Hair on the chin and upper lip only, with clean-shaved cheeks. Excellent for men whose cheek growth is patchy. Balances round and full faces by elongating the chin. Very common in West African men.
Clean shave the cheeks every 2–3 days. Keep the goatee edges sharp. Works well with both fades and longer hair styles.
Stubble
Short, even growth
1–3mm of growth kept deliberately short and even. One of the most flattering beard types for most face shapes. Hides patchiness well and requires minimal maintenance. The right clipper guard makes all the difference.
Use a 1–2 guard for light stubble. Moisturise the skin underneath — not just the beard. Fade into the hairline for a cleaner finish.
Patchy Beard
Uneven growth pattern
Sparse or uneven growth in some areas — very common and almost always genetic. It's not a grooming failure. Most men trying to grow through their patches are fighting something they can't change. Working with the gaps — shaping the beard around your strongest areas — almost always looks better than growing through them.
Keep length short to even out density. Focus shaping on your strongest growth areas. Beard derma rollers and biotin can stimulate sparse areas over time.
Low Fade Beard
Faded into the hairline
The beard fades seamlessly into a low or mid haircut — no clear line between hair and beard. One of the sharpest looking styles for African men. Requires a skilled barber and regular upkeep every 1–2 weeks.
Ask your barber specifically for a beard fade, not just a lineup. The fade should connect through the sideburns and temple. Don't let it grow out more than 10 days or the fade blurs.
Coily / Natural Beard
Unmanipulated coil growth
Letting the beard grow without heat or chemical interference — coils as they naturally form. African coil patterns are some of the most distinct beard textures anywhere. Without consistent moisture they dry out, tangle, and knot — which is where most men give up on this style before it reaches its potential.
Detangle while wet using a wide-tooth comb or brush. Use a leave-in conditioner or beard butter. Avoid over-brushing when dry — it causes frizz and breakage.

Head Shapes

Head shape — what your barber considers first

Head shape determines how a fade looks from the side and back — not just the front. A good barber assesses this before they pick up the clippers. Knowing your own head shape means you can communicate better and understand why certain cuts work on you and others don't.

Round Head
Even curve all around
Evenly curved from all angles with no flat spots. The most common head shape across African men. Cuts sit naturally — the shape is forgiving and works with almost any fade level or top length. Most barbers plan cuts assuming this shape.

Best cuts

  • High fades with medium top length — very balanced
  • Natural afros with even shaping around the perimeter
  • Locs in any length — sit evenly all around
  • Skin fades look especially clean on this shape
Flat Back
Flat occipital region
A flat area at the back of the skull — very common and often unnoticed until a skin fade reveals it. Can make the back of a tight fade look uneven or square from the side. A skilled barber will feather the fade differently to compensate. Tell your barber upfront.

Best cuts

  • Taper fades — softer drop at the back, less exposure of the flat zone
  • Leaving some length at the back to fill and round it visually
  • Locs — add fullness and completely mask the shape
  • Avoid very close skin fades at the back — exposes the flatness
Long / Narrow
Longer front-to-back than side-to-side
Head is noticeably longer from front to back than it is wide side-to-side. Common in East African builds — Somali, Ethiopian, Eritrean frames often carry this shape. Adding width through the sides of a cut balances it; adding only height makes it more pronounced.

Best cuts

  • Medium fades that keep volume through the sides
  • Afros shaped for width, not just height
  • Temp fades or low fades — preserve side width
  • Avoid very short all-over cuts and high skin fades — expose the length
Wide / Broad
Wider than it is long
Head is wider side-to-side than front-to-back. Common in broader West African frames. Adding vertical height through the top of a cut elongates the head shape visually. Skin fades are very effective here because they remove width at the sides.

Best cuts

  • High skin fades — remove side width cleanly
  • Vertical growth on top — flat tops, high-top fades, afros with height
  • Locs worn up or piled high — add vertical presence
  • Avoid cuts that add maximum volume at the sides

Accessories

What to wear with your hair — accessories that actually fit

Most hair accessory guides are written for straight or loosely wavy hair. This one is for natural hair, locs, and African head shapes — which have different weight, grip requirements, and proportions.

Head Wraps & Gele
All hair types · Women
Ankara and Aso-oke head wraps are the most versatile accessory for African hair. They work on every texture from 3a to 4c, protect hair on lazy days or protective style transitions, and can be styled dozens of ways. Gele is the structured formal version for events.

Works best for

  • 4c and 4b hair — protects and adds visual drama
  • Transitioning hair — hides two textures during the grow-out
  • Bad hair days — always looks intentional
  • Any face shape — can be tied to add or reduce height
Durag & Wave Cap
Short hair · Men · Waves
Essential tool for 360 waves — not just a style accessory. The durag compresses the hair against the scalp after brushing, locking in the wave pattern. Satin durags are better than velvet for moisture retention and avoiding frizz.

Works best for

  • Short natural hair working on wave patterns
  • After moisturising and brushing — locks in the work
  • Overnight — keeps edges laid while sleeping
  • Round and oval head shapes — the most secure fit
Satin Bonnet
All textures · Women · Night
Not optional. Cotton draws moisture out of African hair overnight through absorption and causes friction that leads to frizz and breakage by morning. A satin bonnet is the cheapest single change you can make to your hair health — and it works immediately from night one.

Works best for

  • 3c–4c textures — highest moisture loss without protection
  • Protective styles — keeps braids and twists fresh longer
  • Wash-and-go styles — preserves curl definition overnight
Loc Jewellery & Cuffs
Locs · Men & Women
Metal cuffs, beads, and wraps for locs — a tradition with roots across West and East Africa. Modern loc jewellery ranges from minimal metal rings to elaborate cowrie shell and bead arrangements. They add visual interest without damaging the locs when fitted correctly.

Works best for

  • Medium to long locs — enough length to hold the piece
  • Starter locs — beads add visual weight to thin new locs
  • Oblong and oval face shapes — draw the eye across the locs
  • Avoid very tight cuffs that compress the loc at the root
Hair Pins & Clips
Natural & relaxed · Women
Bobby pins and hair clips work differently on African hair — the coil pattern means more grip is needed and standard pins can slip. Spiral pins and duckbill clips with rubberised grips are significantly more effective for 4b and 4c textures. Avoid metal pins with rough ends on fine hair.

Works best for

  • Pinning updos and twists in place without slipping
  • Sectioning during wash day
  • 3a–3c hair — standard pins work reasonably well
  • 4b–4c — use spiral pins or large jaw clips instead
Face Cap
Short hair · Men · Everyday
The face cap — snapback, dad hat, or fitted — is the most universal men's hair accessory. Works as protection from sun, wind, and dust. How it sits on your head depends on your head shape and haircut; high fades and low cuts pair cleanest with fitted caps.

Works best for

  • Round and oval head shapes — sits evenly without gaps
  • Low cuts and skin fades — the cleanest pairing
  • Waves — a fitted cap compresses and sets the pattern
  • Avoid very high-top styles — the crown sits too high for most caps
