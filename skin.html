SKIN

Understanding your undertone changes everything — from which colours you wear to which metals you reach for. Most guides use the wrong language for African skin. This one doesn't.

Undertones

It's not about how dark or light you are.

Undertone is about the hue beneath your skin — warm, cool, or neutral. Two women with the same deep skin tone can have completely different undertones. Undertone determines which colours, metals, and makeup shades work for you. Depth (how light or dark you are) is separate.

Warm
Gold, earth, and sun
Yellow, golden, or peachy hues beneath the skin. Very common across West and Central Africa. The skin looks brightest in earthy tones — terracotta, rust, olive, warm browns — and in gold jewellery. Looks washed out in very cool blues and stark silver.
Signs
  • Veins appear greenish on the inside of the wrist
  • Gold jewellery looks more natural than silver on the skin
  • Warm beiges and browns complement rather than clash
Cool
Blue, berry, and ash
Pink, red, or bluish hues beneath the skin. Common in East African skin tones and some deeper West African complexions. The skin looks sharpest in jewel tones — deep blues, purples, emerald green — and in silver and white gold jewellery.
Signs
  • Veins appear more blue or purple on the wrist
  • Silver sits naturally against the skin
  • Bright cool colours pop without washing out
Neutral
Both work. Most things work.
A balance of warm and cool, or neither distinctly. The most forgiving undertone — both gold and silver look good, a wide range of colours work. Common in medium-depth African skin tones. If you genuinely can't decide between warm and cool, you're probably neutral.
Signs
  • Veins appear blue-green — not clearly one or the other
  • Both metals look good without one obviously winning
  • Most colour families are flattering

Method

How to find your undertone

01
The vein test
Look at the veins on the inside of your wrist in natural light. Green-ish = warm. Blue or purple = cool. Blue-green = neutral. Works best in daylight, not artificial light.
02
The white paper test
Hold a plain white sheet next to your bare face. If your skin looks more yellowish or golden against the white = warm. More pink or rosy = cool. Neither clearly = neutral.
03
The metal test
Hold a gold piece of jewellery against your skin, then silver. If gold makes your skin glow and silver dulls it = warm. If silver looks natural and gold looks brassy = cool. Both look fine = neutral.

Most African women test as warm or neutral. Pure cool undertones are less common in West African skin but more common in East African complexions.

Jewellery

Gold, silver, or rose gold — which is yours

Gold
Warm undertones
  • 14k and 18k yellow gold sits naturally against warm skin
  • Heavy chains, gold hoops, stacked bangles all work
  • Avoid: pure silver as the main piece — creates visual contrast that dulls the skin
Silver
Cool undertones
  • Sterling silver and white gold are your metals
  • Thin silver chains, hooped earrings in silver, stacked silver rings
  • Avoid: yellow gold as the main piece — reads brassy against cool undertones
Rose Gold
Neutral / versatile
  • Works across undertones but especially flattering on medium-depth neutral skin
  • Blends warm and cool — the most versatile metal for African skin
  • A safe starting point if you're still unsure of your undertone

Nigerian jewellery tradition is predominantly gold. If you have cool undertones, lean into statement silver pieces — they're less common and more distinctive.

Skin Type

Dry, oily, combination, sensitive — what's yours

Your skin tone tells you what colours to wear. Your skin type tells you what to put on your face. These are different things. Most people know their skin tone — fewer know their skin type. Get this right first and every other product decision becomes easier.

Oily
Signs: shiny skin by midday, enlarged pores, frequent breakouts, makeup slides off
What works
  • Gel or foam cleansers — remove excess sebum without stripping
  • Niacinamide (5–10%) — regulates oil production and shrinks pore appearance
  • Salicylic acid (BHA) — dissolves oil inside pores, reduces blackheads
  • Oil-free, non-comedogenic moisturiser — oily skin still needs hydration
  • Clay masks (kaolin or bentonite) once a week — draws out excess oil
What to avoid
  • Heavy creams and oils as your main moisturiser
  • Alcohol-based toners — strip oil, trigger rebound overproduction
  • Coconut oil on the face — highly comedogenic, blocks pores
  • Skipping moisturiser — dehydrated oily skin overproduces sebum
Dry
Signs: tightness after washing, rough or flaky patches, dull texture, fine lines more visible
What works
  • Cream or oil-based cleansers — don't strip the skin barrier
  • Hyaluronic acid — draws moisture into the skin (apply to damp skin)
  • Ceramides — repair and reinforce the skin barrier, lock in moisture
  • Glycerin — a humectant that attracts water from the air to your skin
  • Shea butter or squalane — rich emollients for deeper moisturising
What to avoid
  • Foaming or sulphate-based cleansers — strip natural oils
  • Benzoyl peroxide unless treating active acne — very drying
  • Fragrance in skincare — irritates and dries already compromised skin
  • Exfoliating too frequently — once a week maximum
Combination
Signs: oily T-zone (forehead, nose, chin) + dry or normal cheeks. Most common skin type in West Africa
What works
  • Lightweight gel moisturiser — hydrates without adding oil to the T-zone
  • Niacinamide — balances oil production and hydration together
  • Gentle foaming cleanser — removes oil from T-zone without drying cheeks
  • Spot-treat the T-zone with BHA, use richer cream on dry areas
What to avoid
  • Heavy creams all over the face — clogs pores in the T-zone
  • Mattifying products over the whole face — over-dries cheeks
  • Ignoring the dry areas — dehydration makes combination skin worse
Sensitive
Signs: redness, burning, or stinging after products; reacts to new things quickly; easily irritated by heat, cold, or friction
What works
  • Fragrance-free, dye-free products — every ingredient is a potential trigger
  • Centella asiatica (Cica) — calms redness and repairs the skin barrier
  • Aloe vera — reduces inflammation, soothes reactive skin
  • Mineral sunscreen (zinc oxide) — less irritating than chemical filters
  • Patch test everything — apply to the inner arm for 24 hours first
What to avoid
  • Fragrance in any form — the single biggest trigger for sensitive skin
  • Physical exfoliants (scrubs, brushes) — micro-tears worsen sensitivity
  • Retinol without building up slowly — start with low concentration twice a week
  • Introducing multiple new products at once — you won't know what caused a reaction
Normal
Signs: balanced moisture, minimal breakouts, no strong oiliness or dryness, skin feels comfortable throughout the day
What works
  • Any lightweight, non-comedogenic moisturiser — maintain what's already working
  • SPF daily — the best anti-ageing tool regardless of skin type
  • Gentle exfoliation (AHA) once or twice a week — maintains texture and glow
  • Vitamin C serum — brightens and prevents uneven tone from sun exposure
What to avoid
  • Over-complicating your routine — if it's working, less is more
  • Skipping SPF — normal skin is still vulnerable to hyperpigmentation from the sun
Ingredients to look for
  • Niacinamide — brightens, controls oil, reduces hyperpigmentation. Works on all skin types.
  • Hyaluronic acid — draws water into the skin. Works on all types but must be applied to damp skin.
  • Ceramides — repair and strengthen the skin barrier. Essential for dry and sensitive skin.
  • Salicylic acid (BHA) — oil-soluble exfoliant. Works inside pores. Best for oily and combination skin.
  • Glycolic acid (AHA) — surface exfoliant. Best for dry and normal skin. Avoid on sensitive.
  • Retinol / Retinoids — speed cell turnover, reduce hyperpigmentation and fine lines. Start slow.
  • Vitamin C — antioxidant, brightens dark spots, boosts SPF efficacy.
  • Centella asiatica (Cica) — calming, anti-inflammatory. Excellent for sensitive and acne-prone skin.
  • Zinc oxide (SPF) — physical sunscreen, gentler than chemical filters. Good for sensitive skin.
Ingredients to be cautious with
  • Fragrance / Parfum — the most common sensitiser in skincare. Always check the label.
  • Denatured alcohol (alcohol denat.) — strips moisture, disrupts the skin barrier over time.
  • Hydroquinone — lightens dark spots but banned or restricted in many countries. Use with medical supervision.
  • Mercury (in unregulated creams) — highly toxic. Found in some bleaching products sold in Nigerian markets. Avoid entirely.
  • Formaldehyde-releasing preservatives — DMDM hydantoin, quaternium-15. Irritants, linked to sensitisation.
  • Coconut oil (on the face) — comedogenic. Fine on the body, clogs pores on most facial skin.
  • High-concentration benzoyl peroxide daily — very drying. Use targeted and only for active acne.
  • Petrolatum / Vaseline on active breakouts — seals in bacteria. Fine as a barrier on healthy skin.
Before you buy a soap or cream
  1. Check the first 5 ingredients — they make up the bulk of the formula. Everything else is present in trace amounts.
  2. Look for "fragrance-free" not "unscented" — unscented products often contain masking fragrance to cancel the smell of other ingredients.
  3. Check for "non-comedogenic" — especially for moisturisers and foundations if you have oily or combination skin.
  4. Avoid anything promising to "lighten" or "even" skin in Nigeria without knowing what's in it — mercury and hydroquinone are common in unregulated products.
  5. Don't buy based on packaging — a cream in a luxury-looking bottle can have the same formula as one in a cheap sachet. Read ingredients, not branding.
  6. Introduce one new product at a time — wait 2 weeks before adding another so you can tell what's helping or causing a reaction.
  7. pH matters for cleansers — skin is slightly acidic (pH 4.5–5.5). Harsh alkaline soaps (most traditional bar soaps) disrupt the skin barrier. Look for gentle, pH-balanced cleansers.
